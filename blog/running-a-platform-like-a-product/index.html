Running a Platform Like a Product

A team I worked with spent the better part of a day standing up day-zero resources for their Terraform state. They created the resources, sorted the access, wired the RBAC and worked out how to bootstrap the thing before they could start making their real resources. It was careful work but none of it needed doing because our platform already offered a Terraform backend in Artifactory that did the same job with a few lines of config and no day-zero chicken-and-egg problem. They just didn’t know it was there.

The platform feature was built, it worked and it was genuinely better than the thing they reached for instead. It just may as well not have existed for that team because communications slipped through the cracks and they had no reason to go looking.

There are two ways a platform loses. One is a value gap, where the thing you built isn’t worth using and no amount of promotion will save it. This is fixed by building something better or retiring what you have. The other is an awareness gap, where the thing is genuinely worth using and people just can’t find the door. This is a communication problem and once you’ve actually built something good it’s the gap you spend most of your time fighting. That fight turns out to be most of what running a platform like a product is and surprisingly little of it is engineering.

The gap is awareness, not value

An awareness gap is easy to miss precisely because the product is fine. Your dashboards look healthy, the thing you shipped does what it says and nobody is complaining about it. However, the cost is invisible and it’s paid entirely by your customers in work they didn’t need to do.

You find out it’s happening from the edges. A team files a request for something you shipped six months ago. Someone builds an elaborate workaround for a problem the platform already solves. A support ticket that looks like a one-off turns out to be the third time this quarter someone’s hit the same wall. It’s tempting to treat those as interruptions to the real work but they are the real work or at least the clearest signal you’ll get about where the platform is failing to land. If you’re watching how teams actually use the platform rather than how you imagined they would then the awareness gaps show up quickly.

So the goal shifts. It’s not enough to build value, you have to surface it and surfacing it is a job in its own right that nobody else is going to do for you.

Docs are part of the product

The most reliable way to close an awareness gap is documentation: write things down and make it clear enough that anyone could follow it. I know that may seem boring but it’s one of the most important parts and one that I particularly take pride in authoring. But to a customer trying to get something done, the docs aren’t an accompaniment to the product; they frequently are the product because they’re the only part of it that person interacts with before deciding whether to bother.

We decommissioned Terraform Enterprise at one point because the value it brought didn’t justify what it cost us to keep. In its place we enabled Terraform repositories in Artifactory and folded that into the wider platform offering. The interesting part wasn’t the swap, it was the migration that lived or died on how well we sold it and how simple the move would be. So I wrote extensively: how to use the new backend, how to move onto it, what changed and why. That guide was as much the deliverable as the backend itself. A migration you don’t document isn’t a migration, it’s an announcement that you’ve broken something and left your customers to work out the rest.

Keep a roadmap that points both ways

For all this talk of communication the plainest tool does most of the work so it’s worth being concrete about what I mean by a roadmap. I’m not picturing a Gantt chart or a licensed planning tool full of swimlanes and story points. I mean something far simpler: a single page your customers can look at, kept current, split into what’s coming and what’s already landed. It doesn’t need to be pretty and it doesn’t need to live in an expensive product; it needs to exist, stay honest and sit somewhere people will actually find it. Read top to bottom it tells a team where the platform has been and where it’s going, and each direction earns its place for a different reason. Though if you want to make a pretty chart then I’m not complaining.

Point it forward

The forward half is about change that hasn’t happened yet. A lot of platform work is planned months out and sits cooking for a long time before anyone feels it and the big ones are deprecations or changes to how the platform fundamentally works. Those are exactly the changes you cannot spring on people.

It’s worth remembering your customers have their own roadmaps. They’re juggling their own priorities and deadlines and the platform is just one of the things they depend on. When you drop a breaking change on them with a fortnight’s notice you’re not just giving them work, you’re derailing whatever they’d already committed to that quarter. Tell them months ahead and the same migration becomes something they can plan for, slot in and absorb without it hurting.

You might worry that publishing where you’re heading commits you to dates you’ll end up missing. It’s a fair concern and the answer is that a rough direction shared early beats a precise surprise every time. A forward view is also how you say “not yet” honestly, which is a thing platform teams have to say constantly. Pointing at the roadmap is a far better answer than a flat no or a vague maybe.

Point it back

The backward half does two jobs. It’s the standing record of what shipped and when, which means the team stuck building day-zero resources might have seen the Artifactory backend land and recognised the door they were about to walk past. It’s also the clearest evidence of what the platform team actually brings which matters more than platform teams like to admit when it’s time to justify the investment and the headcount.

There’s a trap in that second job worth naming. It’s easy for a backward-looking roadmap to curdle into a list of features shipped, a wall of output that says look how busy we’ve been. That’s the wrong language and it quietly contradicts the whole premise because features shipped was never the scoreboard. Adoption was. So the record has to speak in outcomes, in the work you took off people’s plates rather than the tickets you closed. “You no longer have to hand-roll a state backend” says something. “Delivered Artifactory integration” says nothing to the person who has to care.

None of it helps if nobody sees it. A roadmap sitting in a repository nobody opens is the same failure as the backend that team never found. It’s a channel, not an artifact and a channel only works if the message reaches the people it’s for. Whatever form it takes, the test is whether a busy developer who isn’t looking for it still ends up knowing what changed and what’s coming.

Mostly talking, not shipping

When I add all this up, running a platform well turns out to be mostly listening and communicating, and comparatively little of it is shipping. The engineering is the part we’re most comfortable with and honestly the easier half once you’ve decided what to build. The harder discipline is telling people what exists, warning them before it changes and showing them where you’re going, over and over, to an audience that has better things to do than keep up with you.

That’s the part Platform as a Product left for later. A platform earns its adoption by removing real work but it keeps that adoption by making sure the people it’s for never have to wonder what it does. The best feature you’ll ever ship is worth nothing to the team that never hears about it.
