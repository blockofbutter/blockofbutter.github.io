Platform as a Product

I spent a while helping look after a set of internal Terraform modules and almost nobody used them.

I didn’t build them. I inherited them, maintained them and supported them, which is its own kind of education because you end up watching how something actually gets used rather than how it was meant to. The idea behind them was reasonable enough: wrap up common Azure resources so a team could create them in a way that had already passed security. In practice most of the modules wrapped just a single resource and using them added a dependency on something that wasn’t especially well maintained, took flexibility away and using one was genuinely more work than reading the Terraform documentation and writing the resource yourself. The security story didn’t hold up either, because Azure Policy already enforced the same guardrails a layer down. The module’s whole reason to exist was covered elsewhere and what was left was cost.

Eventually we gave consumers permission to edit the modules themselves. We dressed it up as empowerment but really it was us admitting we didn’t want to own them. That should have been the moment we decommissioned them and spent the effort somewhere it mattered.

What stuck with me is that around the same time I was helping run two other platforms that teams genuinely relied on. One let teams deploy their workloads onto shared Kubernetes clusters from a bit of YAML. The other handed teams a secured, ready-to-go Azure subscription to build in. Same kind of teams, same organisation, yet the gap between those two and the modules is the whole reason I think you have to treat a platform as a product.

Your developers are customers

Treating a platform as a product starts with being honest about who it’s for. Your developers are your customers, even if they never chose you and can’t exactly take their business elsewhere. That sounds obvious but it quietly changes the only question worth asking.

And that question isn’t whether the platform works. The modules worked. They did what they said on the tin and they still failed because working was never the bar. Whether anyone chooses to use it is.

Seen that way, adoption is the only honest scoreboard. Not how many modules you shipped, not how clever the engineering or automation was, not how complete the catalogue looked in a status update. A platform nobody reaches for is a failed product even when every part of it works exactly as designed.

Remove more work than you add

The two platforms that worked did something the modules never managed. They removed an entire problem.

One let a team deploy onto shared Kubernetes clusters by onboarding into a Git repository and describing their workload in YAML. Our automation read that, generated the manifests or rendered the Helm charts and the ArgoCD plumbing underneath was ours to run. The team thought about their app and nothing else.

The other gave a team a place to live in Azure. They declared the resources they wanted and got back a secured, segregated subscription: resource groups, subnets with sensible default routes and NSGs, AAD groups wired up with RBAC and PIM, the lot. It covered the full lifecycle too, so offboarding was as clean as onboarding.

Both of those handed a team an outcome. The modules handed a team parts. A module wraps a resource and then leaves you to do the assembly, manage the state, run the plan and own whatever falls out the other side. It was an ingredient when what people actually wanted was the meal.

So that’s the test I’d put to anything a platform offers: does it remove more work than it adds? The good platforms removed an enormous amount and asked for a YAML file in return. In comparison the modules added work. You took on a dependency, you lost flexibility and you did more than you would have done alone. The value was negative before you even counted what it cost us to maintain them.

When the only thing left justifying something is a guardrail, check that the guardrail isn’t already handled better somewhere else. The modules existed to keep people inside what security would allow but Azure Policy did exactly that at the platform level, for everyone, whether they touched a module or not. Building a product to enforce a rule that policy already enforces just means maintaining the cost of that rule twice.

Be the default door, not an optional detour

There’s a less flattering reason the good platforms did well and it’s worth being honest about. They were the only door. You couldn’t get an Artifactory repository without going through the platform. You couldn’t get a subscription, or an Azure DevOps project, without it. The modules, by contrast, were optional, and the thing they were optional against was “just write the Terraform yourself”.

So I have to ask whether those platforms were genuinely good or simply unavoidable because a mandatory front door can hide a mediocre product behind guaranteed usage. Honestly, I think they were good and the evidence is that nobody tried to route around them. The only time someone went the other way was when they didn’t yet know the platform existed or its processes. Nobody who understood the process wished they could go and assemble a governance-compliant subscription, a set of AAD groups and a network by hand instead. You wouldn’t ask hundreds of teams to do that and they wouldn’t want to.

That comparison handed me the distinction I keep coming back to. The modules had a value gap. The good platforms, at worst, had an awareness gap. Those are completely different problems. A value gap means the thing isn’t worth using and no amount of promotion fixes that. An awareness gap means the thing is worth using and people just can’t find the door which is a documentation and communication problem you can actually solve.

So the goal was never to be mandatory. It’s to be the path of least resistance to something people want and the strongest position is both at once: the sanctioned default that people would have chosen anyway. Mandate without value only breeds resentment and quiet workarounds. Value without being the default leaves you sitting there optional and ignored, which is exactly where the modules ended up.

Know when to pack it up

The modules cost real time to keep alive and returned very little, and the honest response to that isn’t to soldier on or quietly pass the burden to someone else. It’s to retire the thing and put the effort where it counts.

Maintenance is opportunity cost. Every hour spent nursing a set of modules almost nobody used was an hour not spent on the platforms teams actually leaned on. Handing consumers edit rights to the modules felt like a fix but it was really a way to dodge the decision. Inner sourcing can be brilliant when you do it from strength, when a thing is loved and you want shared ownership of it. Doing it because you can’t justify maintaining something is a retreat with extra steps and the cleaner answer is to decommission and reinvest.

A platform that won’t let go of its low-adoption corners slowly turns into a junk drawer and then buckles under the weight of maintaining things nobody asked for. Saying no and sometimes saying “not anymore” is part of running it like a product.

A platform isn’t a portal

None of the platforms I’ve talked about had a user interface. No portal, no service graph, nothing that looked like the screenshots you see online. They were Git repositories, YAML, pipelines and automation. The two that removed real work were quietly excellent regardless.

I mention it because it’s easy to look at something like Backstage and assume a platform needs a shiny front end to count. A UI doesn’t make a platform good. It’s also one more thing to build, run and keep alive, often more than the pipelines and config files sitting underneath it. Chasing the interface is optimising for how the platform looks in a demo rather than how much work it takes off a team’s plate and the second one is the only thing your customers actually feel.

Build something they’d miss

Strip all of this back and it comes down to one test. The platforms that lived were the ones teams would have been genuinely upset to lose. The one that died was the one they barely noticed was there.

So that’s what I try to build. Treat your developers as customers, remove more work than you add, be the door they’d choose even when they don’t have to and keep the discipline to retire what isn’t earning its place. The day-to-day of running one well is another beast that I’ll come back to in a follow-up.
